The Victory Conditions of World War III

Draft — please do not distribute

When did World War II begin? Was it in 1931, when Imperial Japan invaded Manchuria? Was it in 1933, when Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany and began dismantling the Weimar Republic? Was it in 1936, when Germany remilitarized the Rhineland and the world looked the other way? Was it on September 3rd, 1939, when Neville Chamberlain sat before a microphone in Downing Street and told the British people that “this country is at war with Germany”? Or was it in 1941, when the bombing of Pearl Harbor finally drew America into the fight? Ask a room of historians and you will not get a single answer. The reality is that the war did not begin on any single date. It emerged, gradually and then suddenly, as the forces that would define it gathered momentum over a decade or more, while ordinary people in many parts of the world went about their lives hoping it would all blow over.

We should keep this in mind, because the same question will one day be asked about World War III. And the answer, I suspect, will be the same: it did not begin on any single date. It is emerging now, gradually, and the forces that will define it are already in motion.

To understand what is at stake, it helps to look at what was really at stake last time. World War II was not merely a war between countries or even alliances of countries. It was a war between three competing systems of government. On one side stood the Western democracies — the free societies, rooted in the traditions of democratic government, individual rights and the rule of law. On another stood the fascist authoritarian states: Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy and Imperial Japan, each built on the premise that the individual exists to serve the state and that a strong leader, unencumbered by the messiness of democratic deliberation, is the surest path to national greatness. And on a third stood the Soviet Union, communist in ideology, totalitarian in practice, offering its own vision of human progress through the abolition of private property and the dictatorship of the proletariat.

At the end of the war, two sides won and one side lost: the fascists. Their system of government was dismantled and replaced, either by free democracy or by Soviet-style communism depending on which army reached you first. What followed was the Cold War, a protracted struggle between the two surviving systems, which ended when the Soviet Union collapsed under the weight of its own contradictions in 1991. For a brief, heady moment it seemed as though the question had been settled once and for all. Francis Fukuyama famously declared the end of history. Free societies had won.

History, it turns out, was not finished with us. The great failure of the post-Cold War era was to assume that the end of communism would magically produce free societies — as though liberty were the default state of human affairs rather than something painstakingly built over centuries. In Russia, the communist apparatus fell but the institutions of freedom never took root. What emerged was not democracy but a strongman state with a democratic veneer, authoritarian in practice, held together by corruption, force of personality and fear. The lesson should have been obvious: you cannot simply remove an authoritarian system and expect freedom to bloom in the rubble. Freedom requires institutions, traditions and habits of mind that take generations to build.

Today there are again roughly speaking three competing systems of government in the world.

The first is the system of free societies, represented at their best by the values enshrined in the United States Constitution and its Bill of Rights: freedom of speech, freedom of assembly, freedom of religion, protection from unreasonable search and seizure, due process of law.

The second is old-fashioned strongman authoritarianism, best exemplified by Russia but practiced in many forms all over the world: in the theocratic regime of Iran, in the bizarre communist monarchy of North Korea, in military juntas and kleptocracies on every continent. Whether the label is Islamic fundamentalism, revolutionary socialism, or the veneer of democracy, the underlying system is the same: corruption, patronage and fear. These regimes are held together not by technological sophistication but by the age-old methods of intimidation and control of natural resources. They are, for the most part, weak — too backward in technology and institutional process to pose a serious threat beyond their own borders.

The third system is something genuinely new, something that did not exist in the 20th century. You might call it Chinese-style techno-authoritarianism: a system in which the state harnesses the tools of modern technology — mass surveillance, algorithmic control of information, social credit systems, digital censorship — not merely to suppress dissent but to make dissent almost impossible. It is authoritarianism perfected by engineering. This is not a replay of the Cold War. The Soviet Union offered a competing ideology that inspired genuine believers, from Parisian intellectuals to Cuban revolutionaries. Techno-authoritarianism does not ask you to believe in anything. It asks only that you comply — that you submit to the overwhelming strength of something that is vastly more powerful than you and not entirely human, from the mechanics of the state to advanced AI and, soon, actual robots.

Those are the forces swirling in today’s world. But to understand what is truly at stake, we need to be clear about what we are fighting for. Not for any particular country, and certainly not for any particular political party. What we are fighting for is a tradition, a line of progress that stretches back not just centuries but millennia, and that represents, in my view, the single greatest achievement of human civilization.

The roots run deep. The Athenians, for all their flaws, planted the idea that citizens could govern themselves. Roman law established the principle that rules should be written down, publicly known, and applied consistently — that the law is not whatever the powerful say it is today. The emergence of Christianity introduced the revolutionary claim that every human soul has equal dignity before God, a claim whose political implications are still unfolding two thousand years later. These are not English ideas. They are not Protestant ideas. They are not the ideas of any one civilization. They are human ideas, and they belong to everyone.

But ideas do not become institutions by themselves. The particular genius of the English-speaking tradition was to take these ancient principles and, over centuries of struggle, embed them in law and custom. The Domesday Book of 1086 established the principle that the state should know what it governs and govern by record rather than by whim. The Magna Carta of 1215 established that even the sovereign is subject to the law. The Elizabethan religious settlement introduced the then-radical notion that the state should not peer too closely into men’s souls. The English Bill of Rights of 1689 and the Glorious Revolution established parliamentary sovereignty and the principle that power derives from consent.

There is something about the English language itself that is bound up in this. English is a curiously direct language. Its most basic words — cup, book, house, duck, bread, love — are words of one syllable, of Anglo-Saxon rather than Norman origin. It is a language of common people, of Hobbits not Elves. When William Tyndale translated the Bible into English in the 1520s, an act for which he was executed, he put the word of God into the hands of ordinary men and women who could read it and judge its meaning for themselves, without a Latin-speaking priesthood to interpret it on their behalf. The Reformation was many things, but at its core it was an act of comprehension: the insistence that ordinary people are capable of understanding the most important things, and that any institution that denies them this understanding is exercising a form of tyranny.

This principle — that the rules by which people live should be comprehensible to the people who live by them — runs like a thread through the whole arc of progress. The founding of the American republic and the drafting of its Bill of Rights codified individual liberty as the organizing principle of government, in plain language that a farmer could read and understand. The American Civil War established that those principles apply to all people regardless of the color of their skin. The defeat of Nazism and fascism established that those principles are worth dying for. The women’s suffrage movements on both sides of the Atlantic established that those principles apply to all people regardless of sex. The Civil Rights Act established that those principles must be lived and not merely professed.

This is what progress means. Not the partisan label it has become, stripped of content and attached to whatever cause happens to be fashionable this week. Real progress, the kind that matters, is the slow, painful, centuries-long expansion of human liberty — which is not the same as libertarianism — and the constraint of arbitrary power. It is the Whig view of history, and it is the right view, because every step along that line was purchased in blood by people who understood what they were fighting for.

And if that thread has a common enemy, it is the claim — made in every era, by priests, by aristocrats, by commissars, by technocrats — that ordinary people cannot be trusted with comprehension. That the important things are too complicated for common folk. That the meaning of plain language must be decoded by experts. That people should not trust their own eyes and ears. As Hannah Arendt understood, the ideal subject of totalitarian rule is not the convinced ideologue but the person for whom the distinction between fact and fiction, true and false, no longer exists. Every form of authoritarianism begins by undermining ordinary people’s confidence in their own judgment.

I was canvassing door to door in the outskirts of Reno, Nevada in 2016 when I met a man named Paul, a union sheetmetal worker. I stood there on his doorstep and he told me that for the first time in his adult life starting in 1992, he was going to vote Republican for president. I asked him why. He said he had been browsing channels one night and saw this man with orange hair and thought: “Him! I like him!” I pressed him on the appeal. He said it was simple: He could understand what the man was saying.

One may think what one likes about the man with the orange hair. But Paul’s instinct was sound. When the language of governance becomes so ornate, so layered with euphemism and jargon and legalistic abstraction, that an intelligent working man cannot follow what his leaders are telling him, something has gone badly wrong. It is as if the over-educated elites of Davos have regressed to speaking in Latin, and the people, much as they did five hundred years ago, are hungry for someone — anyone — who will speak to them in plain English. The danger, of course, is that dismantling one corrupt priesthood does not guarantee a virtuous one. Henry VIII broke with Rome for a variety of reasons, but helping his ordinary subjects choose their own priests and find their own way to God were not among them. Yet the break mattered. The English Reformation unleashed forces that Henry neither intended nor controlled, and the world was changed for the better because of it. Whether the current populist upheaval produces a Martin Luther or merely more Henry VIIIs remains to be seen. But the hunger it expresses — the demand to be spoken to plainly, to be trusted with comprehension — is legitimate, and any political movement that dismisses this desire does so at its peril.

That is what we are fighting for. A tradition built over millennia, purchased in blood, still unfinished.

So far, so simple.

But here is where things get complicated.

For who, exactly, are we that are doing the fighting? And who are we fighting against?

It is tempting to draw the lines neatly. On one side, the free societies. On the other, the authoritarian regimes. And there are moments when the picture really does look that clear. The protesters of Hong Kong who sang the American national anthem as they faced down armored vehicles. The women of Iran who risk everything by removing their headscarves. The Ukrainian soldiers who die defending their country’s right to choose its own future. The brave Chinese citizens who stood in the streets holding blank sheets of white paper — saying everything by saying nothing, because even silence can be an act of defiance when the state has claimed ownership of every word.

These are rousing images. They are real. And to some degree the story they tell is right.

But mainly it is wrong. Or rather, it is dangerously incomplete.

The question of who is a friend of freedom and who is an enemy does not run neatly along lines of nationality, religion, language, or even job description. Consider the intelligence officer working for MİT, Turkey’s external intelligence agency, supporting the fall of Assad in Syria. Is he a friend of freedom, bringing down a brutal regime that terrorized its own people, or an enemy of it, interfering with a foreign country’s right to self-determination? Or what about a North Korean administrator who risks his life to bend a rule in a small act of kindness, an invisible act of human decency committed from within the belly of a totalitarian machine. Is he not, in his own quiet way, advancing the cause of human liberty more meaningfully than a thousand protesters waving flags for the cameras? What about the FISA judge who denies an application for surveillance because the evidence is insufficient? Is she not defending freedom, from the clandestine authority of a secret judicial bench? And what about the FISA judge who grants one, because the evidence is sufficient and the circumstances demand it? Is she not defending freedom too, from a different kind of threat?

These are uncomfortable questions.

They are intended to be.

The people who advance the cause of freedom are not always the ones holding up inspiring signs for the cameras. They are sometimes the ones who, by the nature of their work, can never be photographed at all. And conversely, the people who undermine freedom are not always foreign dictators or uniformed thugs. They are sometimes legislators drafting well-intentioned bills, executives writing corporate policy, university administrators convening disciplinary panels, software engineers writing algorithms that optimize for engagement, sometimes our own friends and families trying to help — people who would be insulted by the comparison to authoritarianism, and yet whose work, in its cumulative effect, does the authoritarian’s job.

Picking apart who is a friend of the cause of freedom and who is an enemy is not so straightforward.

And as if that were not complicated enough, there is the battle we all face in our own lives. The parent of a teenager, deciding how much freedom to give — knowing that too little crushes the spirit and teaches submission, while too much may invite catastrophe. The schoolteacher who must exercise authority over children who are not yet ready to exercise it over themselves, and who must somehow do so without teaching them that authority exists to be obeyed without question. The friend watching someone they love destroy themselves with drink or drugs, torn between the duty of care the friendship entails and the knowledge that every human being has the right to make their own choices, including terrible ones. These are not just political questions. They are human questions, and they have no clean answers. Anyone who thinks the answers are simple has not thought about them hard enough, and it is precisely those people who are in the greatest danger of doing unintended harm.

So the lines are blurry, and there is no escaping that. But nonetheless let us, with that caveat firmly in mind, try as best we can to examine where the battle lines actually lie.

We will start with the contest that is easiest to see and understand: the one between systems.

The first of these is with old-fashioned strongman authoritarianism. The most glaring of these is Russia, whose war in Ukraine looks less like the warfare of a major power and more like a prolonged campaign of insurgency and attrition, and this pattern of aggression — from the poisoning of Alexander Litvinenko on British soil to the annexation of Crimea to the deployment of mercenary forces across Africa — has been going on for years. Regimes organized along these lines, and there are many, are capable of enormous destruction. But there is cause for optimism, because history suggests that strongman authoritarianism is fundamentally brittle. Suharto fell. Pinochet faced trial. Brazil’s military regime gave way to democracy. Strongman regimes are held together by the strong man. When he goes — and they always go in the end — the whole edifice tends to go with him, sometimes overnight.

The case of Iran is somewhat different, but only in degree rather than in kind. In most strongman regimes, the edifice is held together by one man and his inner circle, and when he falls, the entire edifice falls with him. In Iran, the strong man sits atop a deeper bench of true believers — the Guardian Council, the Assembly of Experts, the senior ranks of the Revolutionary Guard — men who are not merely opportunists clinging to power but genuine ideologues committed to the theocratic project. This makes the task harder. It is not enough to remove one figure; the entire upper architecture of the regime must go. But the fundamental mechanics is the same. Below that layer of true belief lies a vast population — young, educated, connected to the outside world, visibly hungry for freedom — held in place by force.

Whether the regime depends on a single strongman or a group of them, victory over this kind of authoritarianism looks largely similar: containment, support for the brave people who risk everything inside those countries, the patience to wait for brittle things to break, the readiness to be there to support what comes after, and the open-mindedness to recognize that not everyone is going to embrace freedom in the same way. The example of Syria comes to mind.

But China is a different matter entirely, because it represents a different type of system, and it demands a different kind of thinking.

China is not the wealthier cousin of the Soviet Union, nor is it the traumatized rump of the Cultural Revolution and all the ghastly nightmares that evokes, nor is Xi Jinping a strongman leader ruling by fear and patronage alone. China today is safe from crime, which is itself a form of liberty. The streets are clean. The infrastructure is not just functional but inspirational. The food is extraordinary. People go about their lives, raise their families, build businesses, enjoy themselves. There is a genuine sense of opportunity and optimism about the future. There is a quality of daily life in Chinese cities that puts many Western cities to shame. These are real achievements, and dismissing them is both dishonest and strategically foolish. You cannot defeat what you do not understand, and you cannot understand what you refuse to see.

Victory over techno-authoritarianism will not come from waiting for the system to crack. It is adaptive, sophisticated, and in many ways effective. It will be overcome only by a rival vision of human society that is genuinely more compelling — not just richer, but deeper, more free, more inspiring, more worthy of the human spirit. This is an important topic, and I will return to it later.

But before that, it will be worth us taking a careful look at the trend lines in the so-called free world.

I am afraid it is not an encouraging picture.

Consider the United Kingdom. A country that gave the world the Magna Carta, the English Bill of Rights and the idea that even the King is subject to the law has in recent years passed the Online Safety Bill — a piece of legislation that grants the government sweeping powers to regulate what its citizens may say and read on the internet. The British state now routinely not just monitors but regulates the communications of its own citizens on a scale that would have made the Stasi blush.

Or consider what happened during COVID-19. Across the Western world, governments deployed the tools of techno-authoritarianism with remarkable speed and remarkably little resistance. Lockdowns enforced by surveillance technology. Digital vaccine passports determining who could enter a shop or board a train. Social media platforms, acting in concert with governments, silencing dissenting voices — including acclaimed scientists — who questioned the orthodoxy. Emergency powers assumed with the promise that they would be temporary, as emergency powers always are, and relinquished with reluctance, or not at all. The pandemic did not create these tendencies. It revealed them. It showed how quickly a free society can adopt the methods of an authoritarian one when sufficiently frightened, and how few institutional safeguards exist to prevent that.

Is this the behavior of free societies? Or is it the behavior of societies drifting, slowly and perhaps without realizing it, towards the very techno-authoritarianism they ought to be opposing, because they have not even recognized the threat?

The danger is not necessarily a foreign army banging on the door with actual weapons. Nor is it a democratically elected leader who is, to borrow a phrase, definitely not the right sort of chap. Democracies have survived bad leaders before. They are designed to survive bad leaders. The system is built precisely so that the wrong sort of chap can be voted out, mocked in the press, hauled before a court and held to account, or simply allowed to drift into the past as a new national zeitgeist takes precedence. Even uncouth ruffians are entitled to political representation, at least some of the time. That is the whole point of democracy. It is a feature, not a bug.

The more insidious danger, the one we can barely even see, is the steady expansion of surveillance and control carried out in the name of safety, enabled by technology, and subject to almost no meaningful democratic oversight. It is the creeping conviction, shared not just by bureaucrats and technocrats but increasingly by ordinary people, that people cannot be trusted with too much freedom — that speech must be curated, information filtered, behavior nudged, and dissent managed. Quietly. For their own good.

This kind of authoritarianism is not exclusively, and perhaps not even mainly, the province of the state. It is practiced just as readily by corporations, universities, and private individuals who have accumulated power without accountability. The quasi-legalistic disciplinary procedures that have proliferated in companies and on campuses — proceedings in which the accused may have no right to representation, no right to confront their accuser, no clear standard of evidence and no meaningful right of appeal — are as much a threat to individual liberty as anything a government does. When a person can lose their livelihood not because they broke the law but because they violated some vaguely worded policy interpreted by an unaccountable committee, the effect is indistinguishable from state corruption and abuse of power. It is, in many ways, worse, because there is no court to appeal to and no constitution to invoke.

The university professor who destroys a student’s career on a whim, the newspaper owner who suppresses a story to protect a friend, the employer who fires a whistleblower — these are ancient forms of tyranny, older than any algorithm. But what technology has added is something new and in many ways more unsettling: the disappearance of the human being from the exercise of power entirely. A YouTube channel built over years, demonetized overnight by an algorithm, with no explanation and no appeal. A social media account shadow-banned — its owner’s words quietly hidden from the world without their knowledge — by a process that offers no accuser to confront, because there is no accuser, only a system. The feeling is not of being persecuted by a person but of being processed by a machine.

And here is the danger of technological authoritarianism in all this: it is not that it replaces human tyranny, but that it normalizes the unaccountable exercise of power. Living under constant algorithmic surveillance and control dulls our sensitivity to all forms of abuse. It puts us into a stupor, a learned helplessness, where we turn left or right when the phone tells us to but cannot read a map. And from that stupor we become vulnerable not just to the machine but to every petty tyrant who operates in its shadow.

Government is not necessarily the enemy in this regard. Indeed, in many cases it is the remedy — the antitrust action against the monopolist, class-action lawsuit against the social media company, the enforcement of rights that no private entity would voluntarily respect. The question is not state versus individual. The question is whether power — wherever it resides, in government, in corporations, in universities, in intelligence agencies, in social movements, in garden-variety criminals, sometimes within our own friends and families — is subject to constraint, transparency and accountability from competing sources of authority. Where it is, you have a free society. Where it is not, you have authoritarianism, regardless of what flag flies outside the building.

Everything I have said so far probably makes me sound a lot like a standard civil liberties advocate. Privacy is good. State surveillance is bad. Leave people alone. But the reality is more complicated than that, and if I stopped here I would be doing the subject a disservice.

The trouble is that the world is complex, complex questions rarely have simple answers, and absolutism, whether of privacy, free speech, or anything else, creates its own set of chains.

And this means accepting something that many people find uncomfortable: not all parts of a free society are free. There are vocations that demand a surrendering of many of the freedoms we associate with liberty. The armed forces are top-down, hierarchical and secretive by nature, and there is no way around that. The intelligence services ask of people not merely that they risk their lives, as the military does, but that they live a strange and morally disorienting life — doing things that defy conventional morality, and never speaking of them. Even high political office demands its own sacrifices of privacy and autonomy. How can a free society tolerate such corners of authoritarian necessity within itself?

The answer is with difficulty. But such is life.

The key distinction — the one that separates a free society from an authoritarian one — is that in a free society you at least have the option to live freely. People who enlist in the armed forces, allow themselves to be recruited into the intelligence services, or run for high office, voluntarily give up some of their freedom. But nobody forced them to do it. The choice was theirs. And the best way to protect the freedoms of ordinary people, while recognizing that difficult and morally ambiguous work must be done on their behalf, is in my view to keep the two worlds separate. The free world and the necessarily unfree world that defends it must not bleed into one another.

There is an enormous difference, for example, between an intelligence agency monitoring communications behind an impenetrable wall of secrecy in order to prevent a catastrophic attack like 9/11, and the fruits of that surveillance then showing up as evidence in an ordinary courtroom against an ordinary citizen. The first is an uncomfortable but defensible necessity: a free society defending itself by means it cannot fully discuss. The second is the machinery of secret power reaching through the wall and into the life of a person who never consented to be part of that world — who never enlisted, never signed the oath, never agreed to play by those rules. Once that wall is breached, once the tools of the shadow world become tools of ordinary governance, the distinction between a free society that employs secret services and an authoritarian state that employs secret police begins to dissolve. And it dissolves not with a dramatic act of tyranny but with a series of reasonable-sounding exceptions, each one a little wider than the last, until one day the wall is gone and no one can quite remember when it came down.

This separation of the necessarily secret, unfree part of society and the free part has a practical consequence that may seem paradoxical. One of the most effective constraints on the scale of surveillance and covert activity is the discipline of secrecy itself. If intelligence work must be kept behind an impenetrable wall of secrecy — where ordinary people must never know the details beyond what they see in movies about James Bond or Jason Bourne — then the infrastructure cannot grow too large, because large things leak. A small, tightly controlled apparatus can keep its secrets. A sprawling bureaucratic empire cannot. Secrecy, properly maintained, is not the enemy of freedom. It is one of its guardians, because it forces the secret world to remain small and disciplined enough to be contained.

This places an enormous burden of responsibility on those who work behind that wall. The very secrecy that protects ordinary people also removes the normal mechanisms of accountability — the courts, political oversight, the scrutiny of the press. What standard, then, can we expect of those people? Not that they apply conventional morality to every decision. They live in a world where lying is tradecraft and killing can be excused as risk mitigation. But that does not mean there are no standards.

The most important, in my view, is seriousness.

Hannah Arendt, writing about the trial of Adolf Eichmann, identified what she called the banality of evil — the discovery that the architect of genocide was not a monster but a bureaucrat, a man who had simply stopped thinking about what he was doing. He filed the paperwork. He followed the procedures. He went home. The horror was not in his malice but in his absence of seriousness. The photographs from Abu Ghraib told the same story decades later: soldiers posing with naked, humiliated prisoners, not in the service of any intelligence objective, not after any careful weighing of necessity, but out of sheer casual cruelty.

It was all a bit of a lark.

This is precisely the danger in any institution that operates beyond the reach of ordinary accountability: not that the people within it become villains, but that they become careless. That the work becomes casual. That decisions carrying the fate of people’s lives are made with the gravity of a Tuesday afternoon coffee meeting. The people behind that wall must be public servants of the highest caliber — people who treat the gravity of their work with deadly seriousness — not people who have lost sight of why it matters, or who came in pursuit of power rather than the purpose it serves.

And from what I have observed, that is not always the case.

I was in a bar in Georgetown not long ago — the neighborhood of Washington, DC known for its embassies, its diplomats and its spies. At the bar was a group of about eight men playing pool, all speaking with British accents, all in white shirts. One was somewhat older than the rest, standing a little taller, wearing a jacket over his shirtsleeves. I went up and said hello, poked a few jibes about Sir Humphrey, a character from a 1980s sitcom about the British establishment, and asked what they did. Diplomats, apparently. At least, that is what they said.

I studied these men for some time. They ordered beer, and more beer. Then shots, and more shots. One remarked that a lot of Brits frequent this bar — not surprising, given its proximity to the British embassy. Another said: “Maybe we should pretend it’s four hundred years ago and claim it.”

I do not doubt that these men are individually decent people. But the culture they inhabit — the casual swagger, the colonial humor, the sense of the whole thing being fun and games — is not a culture of people who carry the weight of their responsibilities with the seriousness those responsibilities demand, representing their country by getting drunk at a bar on a Thursday night.

It may seem that this is just a story about one evening at one bar. But there are other stories I could tell which would make the same point. The problem is not in any one story about any one person. It is a culture of carelessness, bravado and incompetence, and for every one of these people approaching the end of his career, there are many more coming up behind.

These are the wrong sort of chaps.

And the trouble is that these are not people who post to X in the middle of the night. They do not attract outcry from domestic politicians or foreign leaders. They are invisible, operating behind the very wall I have just argued must remain impenetrable.

This is a very serious problem.

But what is the solution?

I think it is something to do with religion.

Or perhaps a certain kind of spirituality, hard to describe, but you know it when you see it.

As an American friend of a rather Calvinist outlook once told me: “I don’t know if I’m going to heaven, but my woodstack sure looks nice.” The Calvinist cannot know whether he is among the elect. There is no priest to grant absolution, no external confirmation, no certainty. And so the proof is in the work itself — the seriousness, the diligence, the refusal to cut corners even when no one is watching. Max Weber understood this as the foundation of the Protestant work ethic, and it is no accident that the United States, a country steeped in that ethic, has generally maintained a high standard of professionalism in its intelligence institutions. When that standard is upheld, the system works. When it slips — when the work becomes casual, when the gravity is lost, when the people behind the wall forget that they are there to protect more than their own jobs — that is correlated not with any specific scandal but the inevitable decline of the country itself. Romans turning into Italians.

And when the standards slip not in one individual but across an entire institution, the apparatus of surveillance and covert activity, no longer constrained by the discipline of seriousness, grows beyond what secrecy can contain. The infrastructure escapes into public knowledge, not because any individual chose to betray it, but because it became too large and too careless to keep its own secrets. This is what happened in the years before Edward Snowden and John Kiriakou made their revelations. The surveillance state had grown so enormous that leaks were not a matter of if but when.

I should be clear about what I think of those men, because it bears directly on my argument. I know many thoughtful people who regard them as heroes, and I understand the instinct. What they revealed was true, and much of it was genuinely concerning. The surveillance apparatus had grown beyond reasonable constraint. Torture had become not an agonized exception in desperate circumstances but something routine — bureaucratized, procedualized, just another day at the office. The banality of evil had set in.

Snowden and Kiriakou diagnosed the disease correctly. But diagnosing a disease does not entitle you to spread it.

What their revelations accomplished was not reform but contamination. Snowden did not shrink the surveillance state. He dragged ordinary people into its twisted psychology. He put hundreds of millions of people into a state of anxiety and mistrust — the feeling of being watched, of having no privacy, of being subject to an unaccountable power — which is precisely the condition that authoritarians cultivate. Kiriakou did not end torture. He took what should have remained behind the wall — dealt with there, reformed there, punished there — and placed it in the minds of ordinary people who had no power to change it, only to be frightened by it. They blurred the line between the necessarily unfree part of our society and the free part. And once that line is blurred, it is very difficult to restore.

For these reasons, I believe both these men belong in prison for the rest of their lives for what they did, and I believe that any true champion of freedom would willingly accept that punishment.

Can I be accused of a contradiction here? How can I argue, as I have, for addressing people plainly and trusting them with comprehension, while also arguing that they should be kept entirely in the dark about certain things? One answer is that plain speech and secrecy are not opposites. You can address people plainly and, in that plain language, tell them: this is not for you. Carry on with your lives. It is being handled, seriously and carefully, by people who have volunteered for that burden. If you want to do that sort of work and see a little of what is behind the curtain, here is the application form, but be careful what you wish for. For now, your responsibility is to be judicious in who you elect to high office, and that is that.

But the deeper answer is this:

Yes, there is a contradiction, and I do not think it resolves neatly. Perhaps that is itself the point. Authoritarian systems, whether in service to a person or an ideology, have the luxury of consistency — one authority, one answer, no tension. The price of freedom is living with contradictions that do not go away: privacy and security, secrecy and transparency, the duty to act and the humility to doubt. If that sounds uncomfortable, it is. But I for one would rather live with honest contradictions than dishonest dogmas.

And just as not every part of the democratic world is free, not every part of the non-democratic world is unfree. I lived in Vietnam for a time, teaching mathematics at Thai Nguyen University, north of Hanoi. Vietnam is on paper communist — the hammer and sickle fly from every government building — and yet what I discovered is that the Vietnamese are a rather pragmatic people, with a take on Buddhism that has something almost Anglican about it: a quiet tolerance that coexists cheerfully with the official ideology without taking it too seriously. There was no cultural revolution in Vietnam and Ho Chi Minh modeled his declaration of independence from French colonial rule on the American one.

And beyond politics and history, Vietnam is friendly and safe — far safer, in its own way, than many American cities. Shortly after I arrived I discovered the best way to get around town was to hitch-hike on the back of mopeds. You simply stand by the road, flag someone down, and hop on. On one of my first rides, back to the university where I was teaching, the driver indicated after dropping me off that I should give him some money. He spoke no English and I at the time spoke no Vietnamese. Having just been to the cash machine I gave him the smallest note I had, which was 100,000 Vietnamese dong — about five dollars. At this point I thought it might be the part of the story where I get robbed. Instead he looked at it, put it in his wallet, carefully counted out 80,000 in change, and bade me farewell.

The right sort of chap.

Or consider how the Vietnamese feed themselves. In the evenings, all around town, people simply open up a little shop outside their homes — a few plastic stools, a gas burner, a handwritten sign — and sell whatever they make well. Bánh mì here, phở there, tea on the corner. No zoning laws. No health inspectors. No licensing fees. No app. Just a person who makes good soup and a neighbor who wants to buy it. It is, in many ways, a form of economic freedom that was regulated out of the lives of most westerners long ago.

So the free world is partly unfree, and the unfree world partly free.

It is not the case that any one country is good and another is bad. The point is that there is a set of principles — freedom of speech, freedom of conscience, the right to privacy, the rule of law, the constraint of arbitrary power — that are indeed articulated most clearly and with greatest historical effect in the US Constitution. And these principles are indeed worth fighting for not just to defend them but to expand them, not just in our own countries but everywhere.

And this, to my mind, suggests something that may seem counterintuitive. Rather than merely defending the freedoms we have, we should be issuing a challenge.

Around the time of the American Revolution, a group of colonists looked at the English tradition of liberty — parliamentary sovereignty, the common law, the rights of Englishmen — and said to themselves and the world: we can do better. They took those ideas and built something new on top of them, a system of checks and balances, a written constitution, a bill of rights that constrained not just the sovereign but the government itself. The new rising power built on what the old power had achieved, and surpassed it. It was not a rejection of the English tradition. It was its greatest vindication.

America’s founding was noble in its aspirations but deeply flawed in its practice. Slavery and the genocide of Native Americans stand as the most egregious examples, but they are not alone. The point is not that America achieved perfection. It did not, and has not. The point is that the idealism counted for something. It provided a direction to orient towards, a standard against which the nation could be held to account, and against which it has been held to account, painfully and repeatedly, ever since. There is nothing contradictory in aspiring to build a free society while acknowledging that you have not yet built one. That tension between aspiration and reality is the engine of progress itself.

So here is the challenge, and it is directed at China: if you want to be the next great civilization, then be great in all dimensions. Build the world’s finest universities — not just in engineering and science, but in the humanities, the arts, the disciplines that teach people to think freely and question authority. Produce great works of culture that move the human spirit. And set a new standard of freedom and opportunity, one in which a child born in any rural village has a genuine chance of rising to the highest office in the land.

The baton of progress has passed from Athens to Rome, from Rome to London, from London to Philadelphia. Why should it not pass to Beijing?

And in that idea lies a deeper challenge, one that runs beneath politics and governance. The quality that makes techno-authoritarianism feel not entirely human — the cold efficiency of a system that operates as though the human soul were an engineering problem to be solved — is not merely a feature of the technology. It is the legacy of a specific historical catastrophe.

As a child, I would love listening to the stories of my grandmother who was born and grew up in Peking, as it was then known, herself a third-generation immigrant in China, descended from Protestant missionaries who went native. She spoke of Shanghai, where she lived as a teenager, as one of the world’s great cosmopolitan cities — a melting pot of Arabs, Scandinavians, Europeans and Chinese. When she first came to England in the 1930s, I asked her what she made of it. She pulled a face: dirty, dull, boring, by comparison. That was the China that existed before Mao.

It does not exist any more.

The Cultural Revolution did not just destroy people and institutions. It severed China’s connection to the traditions that had given it depth for millennia — the Confucian emphasis on moral cultivation, on the duty of the powerful to the powerless, on learning and scholarship as the foundations of good governance; the Buddhist and Taoist traditions that taught humility before forces greater than the state. Technological power developed at extraordinary speed into the vacuum that was left. And technology without cultural grounding — power without wisdom — is precisely how you build something not entirely human.

History shows that severance from the past can produce either rebirth or catastrophe. The English Civil War tore England apart, but what emerged was the Glorious Revolution, parliamentary sovereignty, constitutional government, and the return of the King. The Treaty of Versailles left Germany rootless and humiliated, and evil took root in the vacuum.

Totalitarianism has many enemies, but the greatest of these is culture.

China’s path to genuine greatness runs not through more technology but through the recovery of what was lost. Not the Marxist-Leninist overlay that sits uneasily on a civilization thousands of years older than it, but the Confucian, Buddhist and Taoist traditions that gave Chinese civilization its depth long before Mao was born. With power comes responsibility, and the responsibility of a civilization that has risen as fast as China is to rediscover, in something of a hurry, the cultural and spiritual roots that ground power in something more than efficiency.

This is not a Western challenge imposed from outside. It is a Chinese challenge, rooted in Chinese history, and the answer lies within China’s own traditions. It would be arrogant and wrong to say that the last word on freedom has been spoken, or that liberty is the exclusive inheritance of English-speaking Protestants.

The baton of progress has passed from Athens to Rome, from Rome to London, from London to Philadelphia. Why should it not pass to Beijing?

The next great chapter in the story of human freedom can be written anywhere, in any language, under any flag — if the people have the will to embrace it.

Chinese greatness, the kind that endures across centuries and earns the genuine admiration of the world, means greatness in all dimensions: material prosperity, yes, but also intellectual freedom, cultural richness, and the kind of political openness that allows a society to correct its own mistakes before they become catastrophes.

But I am not Chinese.

I am American.

Or at least that is what I have become.

And that means it is my job not just to have ideals and values, but to be willing to defend them. Wars should be fought for ideals and values, but high ideals do not negate the necessity of hard power. They demand it. A principle that one is unwilling to fight for is not a principle but a preference.

Let me then ask a blunt question in the plain manner I have been advocating, because at the time of writing there is a question that sits like an elephant in the room:

Should America go to war with China to defend Taiwan?

Opinions of course differ, and rightly so. A war between such major powers is not something to be undertaken lightly. But in my view Taiwan must be defended, even at great cost in blood and treasure, even at great risk of things escalating quickly which would be catastrophic for everyone. That is not because Taiwan is a treaty ally in the formal sense, but because a world in which a predominantly free society of twenty-three million people can be swallowed by force is a world in which the arc of progress has been broken. And if matters were to escalate quickly, for example with China launching a tactical nuclear strike on Guam, that would be no time for pontification and navel gazing but rather the kind of brutal retaliation that makes future strikes less likely. Deterrence is not a theory. It is a promise.

And if America does go to war with China, there is no guarantee that war will be won. China has many advantages I do not need to list. And on top of the practical challenges is the fact that free societies that go to war face a danger that authoritarian ones do not: they inevitably fight on two fronts. The first front is the battlefield itself. The second is at home, in their own institutions and societies, where the demands of war press against the very liberties they are fighting to defend. This is not a hypothetical. Abraham Lincoln suspended habeas corpus during the Civil War. Franklin Roosevelt interned over a hundred thousand Japanese Americans during World War II. The Patriot Act, passed in the panicked aftermath of September 11th, authorized domestic surveillance on a scale previously unimaginable in a free society. Perhaps these erosions of freedom were necessary, perhaps not, but the fact is that wars erode individual liberty, sometimes permanently, while it was those very freedoms that made the fight worth having in the first place.

The challenge of World War III, if it comes to open conflict, will be precisely this: to fight with enough ferocity to win while preserving enough restraint to remain worth winning for. Fighting on two fronts is never easy, because each front has a natural tendency to draw resources and attention from the other. A government at war will always argue that security demands sacrifice, and it will often be right, but the sacrifice must not extend to the principles themselves. War is the state of affairs that exists before the maps are redrawn, both politically and ideologically, and this seems to be the season for bold ideas about what the map should look like when the dust settles. But the boldest idea of all may be that a free society can wage war and remain free. That is the second front, and it may be the harder one to win.

What is the timescale for such a conflict? The horrifying prospect of nuclear war between major powers may prevent the kind of large-scale kinetic conflict that defined the 20th century. If so, regardless of what happens in Taiwan, the war may be long — not the years of the world wars but the generations of the Thirty Years’ War, or the centuries-long struggles between Catholics and Protestants, or Sunnis and Shia. A grinding contest of ideas, influence and institutional power, punctuated by localized violence, stretching far beyond any single lifetime.

If this war is to be long — and it may be very long indeed — then being clear about what victory means is not an academic exercise. It is a necessity.

If this war is won — and it is by no means certain that it will be — it will not end with a surrender ceremony on the deck of a battleship.

It will end, if it ends well, with the quiet, boring, magnificent triumph of ordinary people living their lives, speaking their minds, pursuing their vocations, worshipping or not worshipping as they please, connected to the past yet excited about the future, enjoying the riches of technology without being enslaved by it, spending time with those they love — free from the corrupt and unaccountable exercise of authoritarian power, in all its forms, wherever it resides.

That is what victory looks like.

It is a victory for everyone, in every country, of every political persuasion, of every religion and none. It is a victory that everyone can fight for — from leaders of nations to children in the schoolyard.

And the first step toward that victory is recognizing that the war is already underway.
